Growth and Evolution

Economies of Scale

The fall in average costs of production as a firm increases its size.

Average Costs

It is the cost per unit of output and is calculated by dividing the total costs by the total output. Total costs are calculated by adding fixed costs to variable costs.

Internal Economies of Scale

Technical Economies

Large firms can use sophistaicated machinery to mass produce their products. Therefore the high fixed costs can be spread over the large scale of output, thereby reducing average costs.

Financial Economies

Large firms find it easier to borrow massive sums of money at low rates of interest than small firms borrowing less money, which is because they are deemed to be less risky and can provide sufficient collateral.

Managerial Economies

Large firms can hire specialist managers that can ensure higher productivity and efficiency, therefore reducing the average costs.

Specialisation Economies

It is similar to managerial, however it refers to the division of labour rather than the management itself. Workers can be more skilled and experienced in one particular aspect over most workers, thus raising productivity and reducing average costs.

Marketing Economies

Large firms can benefit from lower average costs by selling in bulk, thus benefiting from reduced time and transaction costs.

Purchasing Economies

When large firms buy resources in bulk, they often get discounts that reduces average costs, so the higher the order, they greater fall in average costs.

Risk-Bearing Economies

Firms that have diversified their product lines, can benefit from this as the risks would be spread, therefore not jeopardising the whole business if one product fails.

External Economies of Scale

Technological Progress

The progress in the entire economy of technology increases the productivity of the entire industry.

Improved Transportation Networks

They aid in prompt deliveries and reducing tardiness from workers which would have otherwise negatively affected the productivity.

Skilled Labour Availability

If more skilled labourers are available in an economy, it would cut costs in looking extensively for suitable options, as well as increase the overall productivity.

Regional Specialisation

If a country specialises in one industry or product, it would provide easier access to skilled labour and equipment, therefore reducing average costs. They could also charge higher prices if the country is well known for the products.

Diseconomies of Scale

The increase in average costs of production as a firm increases in size

Internal Diseconomies of Scale

Loss of Control and Coordination

If a business grows too large, it will become very difficult to control and coordinate with the increasing number of departments, therefore reducing productivity and raising average costs.

Overspecialisation

If firms become too specialised, it might cause problems such as boredom of workers, which leads to lower productivity and efficiency.

Bureaucracy

Paperwork, policies, and administration is likely to increase as a firm grows larger, therefore becoming more time-consuming and adds to costs of a business. It might also affect communication.

Complacency

If firms become too cocky and confident, it might lead to slacking and boredom, which reduces productivity, therefore raising the average costs.

External Diseconomies of Scale

Examples:

Increasing Market Rents

This adds costs to the business without actually affecting the output positively.

Higher Wages etc.

Firms might have to offer larger wages and financial rewards if there are too many competitors, to keep their workers and possible gain new ones from the competitors. This raises costs, leading to higher average costs.

Traffic Congestion

If many businesses are located in one area, it might cause heavy traffic, which increases transportation costs among others.

Small vs Large Organisations

How to Measure Size of Business:

  • Market Share
  • Total Revenue
  • Size of Workforce
  • Profit
  • Capital Employed

Large Organisations Benefits:

  • Brand Recognition
  • Brand Reputation
  • Value-added Services
  • Lower Prices
  • Greater Choice
  • Customer Loyalty
  • Economies of Scale

Small Organisations Benefits:

  • Cost Control (can keep costs low)
  • Financial Risk (easier to manage finance)
  • Government Aid (to provide initial support)
  • Local Monopoly Power (may be the only one in a certain location)
  • Personalised Services
  • Flexibility
  • Small Market Size (safe from large competitors)

Growth

Why Do Firms Want to Grow?

  • To employ Economies of Scale
  • To gain a larger market share and market power
  • Survival against competitors who might also grow
  • Spread risks by diversifying into new markets.
  • And of course, to gain higher profits.

Types of Growth

Internal Growth (Organic)

Occurs when a business grows organically, using its own capabilities and resources to increase the scale of its operations and sales revenue. The methods involve:

  • Lower prices to increase sales
  • Effective promotion, to raise awareness and brand image
  • Producing improved and/or better products
  • Sell with a wider distribution network to reach more customers (ex: overseas)
  • Offer credit to incentivise customers to buy more.
  • Increased capital expenditure, such as internal expansion to new locations, or introducing new tech.
  • Improved training and development (T&D) to create more skilled workers
  • Providing overall value for money

Advantages

  • Better control and coordination than relying on external resources
  • Comparatively inexpensive, only require retained profits
  • Maintains corporate culture, avoiding disagreements/incompatibility with external firms
  • Less risky and the easiest option
  • Encourages brand and customer loyalty

Disadvantages

  • Diseconomies of Scale might be triggered
  • Creates a need to restructure, ex: a sole trader becomes public.
  • Dilution of control and ownership, as more shareholders might be involved.
  • Slower Growth, and shareholders might prefer speed over low risk.

External Growth (Inorganic)

Occurs through dealings with outside organisations, such as alliances, mergers, or takeovers. General benefits include:

  • Faster than internal growth
  • Quick way to reduce competition
  • Greater market share and power
  • Businesses can share ideas after merging, creating new ideas and skills.

Merger - Takes place when two firms agree to form a new company
Takeover- Occurs when a company buys a controlling interest in another firm, thus eliminating competition.

Types of Mergers/Aquisitions

Horizontal Integration

The most common type of integration, where two businesses in the same stage of production merge together

Advantages:

  • Increased market share
  • Might enable Economies of Scale
  • Reduces competitors in the industry

Vertical Integration

Where two business in different stages of production merge together. It could either be Forward Vertical Integration (a business merging with another at a later stage of production), or Backwards Vertical Integration (a business merging with another at an earlier stage of production).

Advantages:

  • Assured supply of resources/outlet for product
  • Can prevent selling competitor’s products or supplying them with raw materials
  • Absorbs the profit margin
  • Reliable method of researching consumer tastes

Lateral Integration

Integration between firms that have similar operations but do not directly compete with each other. Ex: Tata acquiring Jaguar.

Conglomerate Integration

When two business in completely different industries merge together.

Advantages:

  • Risks can be spread, so impact of a failure will reduce
  • Ideas can be transferred across the industries

General Advantages of M&As

  • Greater market share
  • Economies of Scale
  • Synergy (access to more resources and ideas when joining two businesses)
  • Survival (to compete with competitors more easily)
  • Diversification (wider customer base and spread risks)

General Disadvantages of M&As

  • Redundancies (surplus of workers result in unemployment)
  • Conflicts due to potential disagreements
  • Culture clash
  • Loss of control
  • Diseconomies of Scale
  • Regulatory problems (Govts want to prevent monopolies from occurring)

Joint Ventures

Occurs when two or more businesses split the costs, risks, control, and rewards of a business project. This means that the businesses come together to create a new legal entity. To clarify, if Business A creates a joint venture with Business B, then they would create a new Business C, while the other two exist as well.

Advantages

  • Synergy (sharing of resources and ideas)
  • Spreading of costs and risks
  • Enable easier entry to foreign markets
  • Relatively cheap (compared to takeovers)
  • Competitive advantages (reduced competition , increased competitiveness)
  • Exploitation of local knowledge
  • High success rate

Strategic Alliances

Quite similar to joint ventures in the way that resources, costs and ideas are shared, however they do not form a separate legal identity.

4 Key Stages to Form SAs

  • Feasibility Study- to investigate the objectives, logic, and feasibility of the SA
  • Partnership Assessment- to analyse the different potential partners and who has the most to offer
  • Contract Negotiation- to mutually accept an offer that benefits both sides
  • Implementation- operations are initiated with commitment to the contract from all parties

Franchising

A form of ownership whereby a person or business buys a
license to trade using another firm’s name, logos, brands and trademarks. In return, the purchaser of the license (franchisee) a license fee to the parent company (franchisor)

Benefits for Franchisor Drawbacks for Franchisor
Rapid growth is possible, without risking high amount of money Huge Risk, as franchisee can damage reputation and image
Don’t pay for running costs such as salaries and rents etc. Difficult to control franchisee to uphold quality standards
Receive loyalty payments from franchisee and in some cases, percentage of sales revenue Although faster than internal growth, slower than M&As
More incentives for franchisee than managers (they keep profits), increasing chances for success. Franchisee keeps all the profits.
Benefits for Franchisee Drawbacks for Franchisee
Lower comparitive risk, as franchise is known and most likely to be successful Restrained by regulations and rules, limiting entrepreneurial talents
Lower start up costs, as ideas and advertisements are taken care of by franchise Can be very expensive, yet no guarantee for success
Franchise will provide added-services to franchisees, eg. training and financial advice Franchisees have to pay share of sales revenue to the franchisor

Globalization

The growing integration and interdependence of the world’s economies, causing national economies to integrate towards a single global economy, where consumers have increasing similarity of habits and tastes.

Opportunities

  • Increases level of competition, reducing wastes and increasing efficiency.
  • Increase in customer base, especially for MNCs.
  • Economies of Scale (global marketing economies and risk-bearing economies)
  • Greater choice of location, helping reduce costs of production, and find a suitable location.
  • External growth opportunities increase
  • Increased sources of finance.

However, there are occasional disadvantages as well, as meeting consumer expectations becomes more and more difficult, and some of these can be seen as threats as well, such as increased competition making survival more difficult, and possibly enabling Diseconomies of Scale.

Multinational Companies

MNCs are organizations that operate in two or more countries. This form is becoming more and more popular for these reasons:

Advantages

  • Increased customer base
  • Cheaper production costs (such as cheap labour and subsidies in some locations)
  • Economies of Scale
  • Avoid protectionist policies such as taxes, tariffs, and quotas.
  • Spread risks

Tariffs- tax on imports, adding price to foreign goods
Quotas- Quantitative limits imposed on the sale of imports, resulting in lower supply.
Restrictive Trade Practices- acts that unfairly, though not illegally, discriminate aginst foreign firms.

A host country is any nation that allows MNCs to set up in its country. These are their advantages and drawbacks:

Advantages

  • Create jobs in the country, reducing unemployment
  • Boosts GDP, therefore aiding in economic growth
  • Introduces new skills and technology to the country
  • Intensifies competition, therefore reducing waste and increasing efficiency.

Disadvantages

  • Can cause unemployment, as they pose a threat to domestic firms, forcing some to close or become less competitive, both leading to job losses.
  • Profits can be sent back/repatriated to the home country, and danger of MNCs suddenly leaving for other countries/locations.
  • Lack of Social Responsibility leading to resource depletion among others.
  • Fierce competitiveness can make domestic firms vulnerable to takeovers as they might not be financially strong enough to compete.
