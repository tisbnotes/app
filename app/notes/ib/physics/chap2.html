Kinematics

Motion

Definitions

  • Distance travelled: the length of the path followed by an object.
  • Speed: the rate of change of distance.
  • Displacement: the change in position of the object.
  • Velocity: the rate of change of displacement.
    • Instantaneous velocity: the speed at any given moment when an object is moving.
  • Acceleration: the rate of change of velocity.
    • Acceleration of free fall (on earth): 9.8ms−19.8 \text{ms}^{-1}9.8ms−1

Dynamics

Definitions

  • Inertia is resistance an object has to a change of velocity.
  • Mass is a numerical measure of:
    1. the inertia of a body.
    2. the amount of matter in an object.
  • Weight is the gravitational force acting on an object.
  • A system is a collection of more than one body, mutually interacting with each other.

Forces

  • Forces have two types:
    • Contact: Forces exerted by objects actually touching each other.
    • Non-contact (long range): Forces exerted without objects touching (gravity, electromagnetic).
  • Equilibrium:
    • Conditions for equilibrium:
      • The net force acting on it is 000.
      • The net moment it experiences is 000.
    • An object in equilibrum might either be stationary or moving at constant velocity.

Tension

  • The force that arises when a body is stretched.
  • A body that is stretched is said to be taut.

Reaction/normal/perpendicular force

  • If a body touches another body, a reaction force exists between the two bodies.
  • The force is opposite to the body exerting the force.

Drag force

  • Forces that oppose the motion of a body through a fluid.
  • Directed opposite to the velocity of a body.
  • ↑\uparrow↑ speed ⇒ ↑\uparrow↑ drag force

Upthrust

  • Upthrust is caused by the pressure that the fluid exerts on the body.
  • If upthrust force=weight\text{upthrust force} = \text{weight}upthrust force=weight:
    • the body will float steadily in the liquid.
  • If upthrust force>weight\text{upthrust force} > \text{weight}upthrust force>weight:
    • The body will float to the top of the liquid or until upthrust force=weight\text{upthrust force} = \text{weight}upthrust force=weight.
  • If upthrust force<weight\text{upthrust force} < \text{weight}upthrust force<weight:
    • The body will sink to the bottom of the liquid or until upthrust force=weight\text{upthrust force} = \text{weight}upthrust force=weight.

Frictional force

  • Forces that oppose the motion of a body in the opposite direction of the force applied.

  • Friction is parallel to the surface, but opposite to the motion that is applied.

  • There are two types of friction:

    • Static friction.
    • Kinetic friction.

    Static Friction

    • This is the force that opposes the start of a motion.
    • The object must start from rest.
    • Static friction has two characteristics:
      1. It is independent of the contact area.
      2. Magnitude is proportional to the magnitude of the normal force FNF_NFN​, which means:
        fsmax∝FNf_s^{max} \propto F_Nfsmax​∝FN​
    • As fsmax∝FNf_s^{max} \propto F_Nfsmax​∝FN​, a proportionality constant μs\mu _sμs​ can be derived:
      fsmax=μsFNf_s^{max} = \mu_s F_Nfsmax​=μs​FN​
    • Where:
      • fsmaxf_s^{max}fsmax​ is the maximum possible magnitude of static friction.
      • μs\mu_sμs​ is the coefficient of static friction.
      • FNF_NFN​ is the normal force.
    • As more force is applied on an object, fsf_sfs​, static friction, will increase upto fsmaxf_s^{max}fsmax​ linearly, after which kinetic friction begins to act on the object once it is in motion.

    Kinetic Friction

    • This is the force between two objects in motion.
    • Kinetic friction, fkf_kfk​, is proportional to FNF_NFN​:
      • fk∝FNf_k \propto F_Nfk​∝FN​
    • A proportionality constant μk\mu_kμk​ can be derived:
      fk=μkFNf_k = \mu_k F_Nfk​=μk​FN​
      • fkf_kfk​ is the magnitude of kinetic friction.
      • μk\mu_kμk​ is the coefficient of kinetic friction.
      • FNF_NFN​ is the normal force.

  • Static friction is much greater than kinetic friction:
    μk<<μs\mu_k < <\mu_sμk​<<μs​

Newton’s Laws of Motion

First Law of motion:

  • Newton’s first law of motion states that the velocity of an object will not change if there is no net force acting on it.
  • So, if ΣF=0\Sigma{F} = 0ΣF=0, velocity remains constant.
  • Translational equilibrium:
    • In simple terms, translational equilibrium is achieved either when:
      • v=0v = 0v=0
      • v is constantv \text{ is constant}v is constant
    • The condition for this is Fnet=0F_{net} = 0Fnet​=0.
    • If the net force acting on an object is zero, the speed and direction of the motion will not change (the object will not accelerate).

Third Law of Motion

Collisions

  • In any type of collision, momentum is always conserved.
  • This is the law of conservation of momentum, which states that the momentum inside a closed system is always constant.

Elastic collisions

  • Kinetic energy is conserved.
  • Final kinetic energy is equal to initial kinetic energy.
  • Ef=EiE_f = E_iEf​=Ei​
  • In an ideal, perfectly elastic collision, there is no net conversion of kinetic energy into other forms such as heat, noise, or potential energy.
  • This is a theoretical concept, and does not take place in the real world.

Inelastic collisions

  • Kinetic energy is not conserved.
  • Ef≠EiE_f \neq E_iEf​​=Ei​
  • Velocity of objects change after collision.
