Government Intervention

Forms of government intervention

  • There are 3 main forms of government intervention:
    • Indirect tax
    • Subsidy
    • Price controls:
      • Maximum price(price ceiling)
      • Minimum price(price floor)

Indirect Tax

  • A tax imposed on the expenditure of goods and services.
  • Can be of two kinds:
    • Specific tax: tax amount per unit does not change with changes in the price of the product.
    • Ad valorem tax/Percentage tax: tax per unit changes with changes in price.
  • The government might choose to impose an indirect tax to:
    • reduce consumption of a demerit good.
    • to earn revenue.
  • The imposition of an indirect tax leads to:
    • Costs of production ↑\uparrow↑
    • Supply curve shifting to the left.

Subsidy

  • A subsidy is a form of financial aid/assistance provided by the government to the producer to reduce the costs of production.
  • The government might choose to give a subsidy in order to:
    • increase the consumption of a merit good.
    • increase the consumption of exports.
    • increase the growth of small industries.
  • Imposition of a subsidy leads to:
    • Costs of production ↓\downarrow↓
    • Supply curve shifting to the right.

Changes in demand and supply equations

  • Suppose a subsidy of SSS dollars per unit is provided by the government.
  • Before provision:

    • Qs=c+dpQ_s = c + dpQs​=c+dp
  • After provision:
    • Qd=a−bpQ_d = a - bpQd​=a−bp
    • Qs=c+d(p+S)Q_s = c + d(p + S)Qs​=c+d(p+S)

Maximum price

  • Also known as a price ceiling.
  • The government can impose a maximum price on the market of a certain good to make it affordable to everyone.

Consequences:

  • Shortage of supply.
  • Black market created. As there is an excess of demand, there exist people who are willing to pay for the good at a price higher than the ceiling price. The government has no control over the quality of goods that are sold in the black markets.
  • Welfare loss created because the decrease in producer surplus is greater than the increase in consumer surplus.
  • Government starts to subsidize the producer.
  • Government becomes a producer itself.
  • Leads to non-price rationing: price does not determine who gets the good as there is a shortage of supply, so those who are willing and able to buy the good might not get it as not many units of the good are available. This may lead to non-price rationing methods, such as:
    • First-come first-serve basis: those who come first will get a chance to buy the good.
    • Distribution of coupons to interested buyers: so they can purchase a fixed amount of the good in a given time period.
    • Favoritism: the producer may choose to provide the good to preferred customers.

Consequences on stakeholders

  • Consumers:
    • Partly gain and lose.
    • They gain because they are getting the good at a lower price than the equilibrium price.
    • They lose as there is a shortage of supply and so not everyone willing and able to buy the good gets it.
  • Producers:
    • Lose.
    • They are forced to sell the good at a lower price than the equilibrium and at a lower quantity.
    • Therefore, their revenue falls.
  • Government:
    • Since the government begins to subsidize the good and becomes a supplier, government expenditure increases.
    • This may lead to an increase in direct taxes that affects standards of living negatively in the long term.

Minimum Price

  • Also known as a price floor.
  • Government may choose to impose a minimum price to increase the revenue of certain goods.

Consequences

  • Excess supply, surplus.
  • Welfare loss created because the decrease in consumer surplus is greater than the increase in producer surplus.
  • May lead to positive advertising of the good by the government.
  • The government becomes a buyer of the good to remove the surplus supply.

Consequences on stakeholders

  • Consumers:
    • Lose.
    • They have to pay a higher price for the good and get to buy a smaller quantity of it.
  • Producers:
    • Gain.
    • They get to sell their product at a higher price and can sell a higher quantity.
    • Thus, increasing their revenue.
  • Government:
    • Since the government begins to buy the good, government expenditure increases.
    • This may lead to an increase in direct taxes that affects standards of living negatively in the long term.
