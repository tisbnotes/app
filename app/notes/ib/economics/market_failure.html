Market failure

Definitions

  • Market failure: when the free market fails to allocate its resources efficiently, the market is said to have failed.
  • Private cost: cost incurred by the individual, firm, or government who is producing or consuming the product.
  • External cost: negative spillover effects/externalities on a third party not involved in the economic activity.
  • Social cost: sum of private and external costs.
  • Private benefit: benefit obtained by the individual, firm, or government who is producing or consuming the product.
  • External benefit: positive spillover effects/externalities on a third party not involved in the economic activity.
  • Social benefit: sum of private and external benefits.
  • Demerit good: a good that has negative externalities of consumption and is over-consumed.
  • Merit good: a good that has positive externalities of consumption and is under-consumed.
  • Public good: a good that is non-rivalrous and non-excludable in consumption.

Causes of market failure

Positive externalities of production

  • A positive externality exists because:
    • At QeQ_eQe​: MPC > MSC.
    • MEC = MSC - MPC
    • MEC is negative.
    • So, a positive externality exists.
  • Example: eco-tourism.

Negative externalities of production

  • Since QeQ_{e}Qe​ > QsoQ_{so}Qso​, there is overproduction.
  • A negative externality exists because:
    • At QeQ_eQe​: MSC > MPC.
    • MEC = MSC - MPC
    • MEC is positive, meaning an external cost exists.
    • So, a negative externality exists.
  • Example: building an airport, building roads, etc.

Positive externalities of consumption

  • Since QsoQ_{so}Qso​ > QeQ_eQe​, there is underconsumption.
  • A positive externality exists because:
    • At QeQ_eQe​: MSB > MPB.
    • MEB = MSB - MPB
    • MEB is positive, so an external benefit exists.
    • So, a positive externality exists.
  • Example: education, vaccinations, etc.

Negative externalities of consumption

  • Since QeQ_{e}Qe​ > QsoQ_{so}Qso​, there is overconsumption.
  • A negative externality exists because:
    • At QeQ_eQe​: MPB > MSB.
    • MEB = MSB - MPB
    • MEB is negative.
    • So, a negative externality exists.
  • Example: drugs, alcohol, cigarettes, etc.

Government responses to negative externalities

Corrective taxes:

  • An indirect tax can be imposed on a demerit good. It drives up the price of the good and reduces the consumption of the good.
  • Evaluation:
    • Advantages:
      • Source of revenue for the government.
    • Disadvantages:
      • Demand for most demerit goods are price inelastic so the percentage decrease in demand is less compared to the percentage increase in price.
      • Higher costs for the producer.
      • Higher price for the consumer, and since the demand is inelastic, the consumer expenditure on the demerit good increases and expenditure on other merit goods decreases, which leads to a lower standard of living.
      • Unemployment might be created.
      • Loss of competitiveness in the global market.

Effect of a corrective tax on a good with negative externalities of consumption

Tradeable permits

  • A government or a multi-national body issues or auctions off permits to polluting industries which allow them to emit only a certain amount of carbon.
  • If firms want to pollute beyond the permitted amount, they will have to buy more permits or reduce their emissions.
  • To acquire more permits, they need to buy them from firms in the market (which do not need their permits anymore).
  • The supply of permits is fixed. The demand determines the price of pollution. The more firms want to pollute, the more expensive the permits become as they need to buy more.
  • There is a strong incentive for firms to reduce their pollution emissions because they can sell the permits they do not need, adding to the firm’s profit.
  • Advantages:
    • Creates a strong incentive to reduce pollution since permits can be sold off for profits.
    • Creates a clear price for pollution, changing the external costs into private costs for the firm.
    • Places a clear limit on the quantity of pollution produced each year.
    • Price of permits can be increased by reducing the supply.
  • Disadvantages:
    • The price of permits is determined by the free market, and may be too low to create a strong incentive.
    • The amount of permits is determined by the government, and may be too high if polluting industries are able to influence the policy.
    • Costly and difficult to monitor industries to make sure everyone who pollutes has the permits to do so.

Ban

  • A ban can be placed on the good to reduce the negative externalities.

Regulations

  • Regulations on factors such as age can be placed to reduce the consumption and reduce the negative externalities of the good.

Government responses to positive externalities

Subsidies

  • Provided to goods that have positive externalities of production.
  • Since these goods are naturally underproduced, the subsidy helps decrease the cost of production and increase the production of the good.
  • Evaluation:
    • Advantages:
      • It can help increase the positive externalities caused by the good.
    • Disadvantages:
      • Difficult to assess the extent of external benefit and external cost due to provision of the subsidy.
      • Externalities of consumption are not taken into consideration.
      • Provision of a subsidy leads an increase in government expenditure, which leads to an increase in budget defecit. The government might try to increase taxes to deal with the defecit and this would reduce the disposable income of families, which will lead to a reduction in consumption and standards of living.
