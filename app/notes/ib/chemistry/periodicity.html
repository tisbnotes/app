Periodicity

Trends in physical properties

Effective nuclear charge

  • The nuclear charge of an atom is given by its atomic number.
    • For example, OOO has a nuclear charge of +8+8+8.
  • The outer electrons of the atom do not experience the full attraction of the nuclear charge because they are shielded from the nucleus because of the repulsion by the inner electrons and so the effective nuclear charge is lesser than the nuclear charge.

  • The effective nuclear charge increases across a period.
  • However, it remains the same down a group.

Atomic radius

  • The atomic radius rrr is half the distance between 2 neighbouring nuclei.
  • For simplicity, however, it is considered as the distance from the nucleus to the outermost electrons of an atom.
  • Across a period:
    • Effective nuclear charge ↑\uparrow↑
    • Attraction between nucleus and outermost electrons ↑\uparrow↑
    • Atomic radius ↓\downarrow↓
  • Down a group:
    • Effective nuclear charge remains the same
    • Principal energy level ↑\uparrow↑
    • Number of occupied electron shells ↑\uparrow↑
    • Distance of outermost electrons from the nucleus ↑\uparrow↑
    • Atomic radius ↑\uparrow↑

Ionic radius

Cations

  • Due to the formation of a positive ion, electrons are expelled from the outermost shell and the outermost shell is lost.
  • Therefore, the ionic radius of a cation is lesser than the atomic radius of the atom.
  • For example:
    • Na\text{Na}Na has an atomic radius of 186×10−12m186 \times 10^{-12} m186×10−12m, but Na+\text{Na}^{+}Na+ has an ionic radius of 98×10−12m98 \times 10^{-12} m98×10−12m.

Anions

  • Due to the formation of a negative ion, electrons are added to the outer shell.
  • This leads to more electron-repulsion between the electrons in the outermost shell, which reduces the attraction between the nucleus and the outermost electrons and so the outermost shell moves further away.
  • This leads to an increased ionic radius.
  • Therefore, the ionic radius of an anion is more than the atomic radius of the atom.
  • For example:
    • Cl\text{Cl}Cl has an atomic radius of 99×10−12m99 \times 10^{-12} m99×10−12m, but Cl−\text{Cl}^{-}Cl− has an ionic radius of 181×10−12m181 \times 10^{-12} m181×10−12m.

Ionisation energy

  • The first ionisation energy of an element is the energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms in their ground state.
  • Across a period:
    • Effective nuclear charge ↑\uparrow↑
    • Attraction between nucleus and the outermost electrons ↑\uparrow↑
    • Energy required to remove an electron ↑\uparrow↑
    • Ionisation energy ↑\uparrow↑
  • Down a group:
    • Principal energy level ↑\uparrow↑
    • Distance between outermost electrons and the nucleus ↑\uparrow↑
    • Attraction between nucleus and the outermost electrons ↓\downarrow↓
    • Energy required to remove an electron ↓\downarrow↓
    • Ionisation energy ↓\downarrow↓
  • Exceptions:
    • Group 2 and 3
      • Elements of Group 3 have a lower ionisation energy than elements of Group 2.
      • Elements of Group 3 have the electron configuration of ns2np1ns^2np^1ns2np1, which means that the electron removed is from the ppp orbital, which is unpaired, making it easier to remove.
      • However, elements of Group 2 have an electron configuration of ns2ns^2ns2, which is a complete orbital and when an electron is removed, it is removed from this orbital, and it is relatively harder to remove the electron.
      • Therefore, the ionisation energy of elements of Group 2 is greater than the ionisation energy of elements of Group 3.
    • Groups 15 and 16:
      • Elements of Group 16 have a lower ionisation energy compared to elements of Group 15.
      • Elements of Group 15 have an electron configuration of npx1py1pz1np_x^1p_y^1p_z^1npx1​py1​pz1​ which means the ppp orbital is half-filled, so it is harder to remove an electron from the orbital.
      • Elements of Group 16 have an electron configuration of npx2py1pz1np_x^2p_y^1p_z^1npx2​py1​pz1​, which means that the electron that is removed gets removed from the pxp_xpx​ orbital which has 2 electrons already, which are repelling each other. This makes it easier to remove the electron from the atom.
      • So the ionisation energy is lesser for elements of Group 16.

Electron Affinity

  • Electron affinity is the energy change when one mole of electrons is added to one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous ions.
  • Electron affinities reach a maximum for Group 2 and Group 15 elements.

Group 2

  • All Group 2 elements have an electron configuration of ns2ns^2ns2, meaning the extra electron added has to be placed in a new orbital which is further away from the nucleus and so experiences reduced electrostatic forces of attraction due to shielding from electrons in the nsnsns orbital.
  • Therefore, the energy is needed to overcome to electrostatic repulsion between the electrons in the nsnsns orbital and the added electron.
  • So, the electron affinity is positive as the reaction is endothermic and energy is required for the reaction.

Group 15

  • All Group 15 elements have an electron configuration of ns2npx1py1pz1ns^2np_x^1p_y^1p_z^1ns2npx1​py1​pz1​, so the added electron has to occupy a ppp orbital that already has a single electron.
  • The added electron repels the electron already in the orbital, which leads to more repulsion and reduced attraction.
  • This means that energy is required for the electron repulsion to be overcome, so the electron affinity is positive as it is an endothermic reaction.
  • Note: electron affinity is negative only for nitrogen.

Electronegativity

  • Electronegativity is the measure of the ability of an element’s atoms to attract electrons in a covalent bond.
  • It is also the measure of the attraction between the nucleus and its outermost electrons, or bonding electrons.
  • An element with high electronegativity has strong electron pulling power.
  • An element with low electronegativity has weak electron pulling power.
  • Across a period:
    • Effective nuclear charge ↑\uparrow↑
    • Atomic radius ↓\downarrow↓
    • Attraction between nucleus and outermost electrons ↑\uparrow↑
    • Electronegativity ↑\uparrow↑
  • Down a group:
    • Atomic radius ↑\uparrow↑
    • Distance between the nucleus and the outermost electrons ↑\uparrow↑
    • Attraction between nucleus and outermost electrons ↓\downarrow↓
    • Electronegativity ↓\downarrow↓
  • Elements with high electronegativity have the most exothermic electron affinities as they do not need any energy in order to add an electron to their shells and release energy when an electron is added.
  • The general trends in electronegativity and ionisation energy are the same.

Trends in chemical properties

Metals

  • Reactivity refers to how easily metals donate electrons.
  • For metals, reactivity increases down a group.
  • Metallic character decreases while moving across a period.
  • The first group elements donate 1 electron from the S orbital, consequently, less energy is required.

  • Down the group, atomic radius increases, which decreases electrostatic forces of attraction, which increases electron mobility.

  • Low densities
  • Have grey shiny surfaces when cutting with a knife.

Non-metals

  • The valence shell is further away from the nucleus down the group.
  • Therefore, reactivity decreases down a group.

Group 7: Halogens

  • The halogens exist as diatomic molecules, bonded covalently.
  • More reactive halogens displace less reactive ones from their ionic compounds.

Properties

  • Coloured
  • They show a gradual change from gases (fluorine and chlorine) to liquid (bromine) to solids (iodine and astatine).

Oxides

  • Change from basic to amphoteric to acidic across a period.
  • When elements bond with oxygen, oxides are formed.
  • Ionic compounds are formed between a metal and a nonmetal so the oxides of Na, Mg, and Al have giant ionic structures. (High M.P)
  • Covalent compounds are formed between non-metals so the oxides of P, S, and Cl are molecular covalent. (Low M.P)
  • The oxide of the metalloid silicon has a giant covalent structure. (High M.P)

Electronegativity and Ionic Character

  • The ionic character of a compound depends upon the difference in electronegativity between its elements.
  • Oxygen has an electronegativity of 3.5 so the ionic character of the oxides decreases from left to right as the electronegativity values of the Period 3 elements approach this value.
  • The oxides become more ionic down a group as electronegativity decreases.
  • Electrical conductivity is a measure of ionic character and can only be measured in the molten state when electrons are free to move.

Acid-Base Character

  • Metallic elements form basic oxides.
  • Non-metallic elements form covalent or non-metallic oxides which are acidic.
  • Na and Mg oxides are basic.
  • Al and Si oxides are amphoteric – they show both acidic and basic properties.
  • PPP, SSS, and ClClCl oxides are acidic.
