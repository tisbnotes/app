Biases in Thinking and decision making

The Dual Process Model

  • Daniel Kahneman proposed the theory of system 1 and system 2 thinking.

    System 1 System 2
    Fast Slow
    Context Dependent Abstract
    Impulsive and effort less Conscious and demands effort
    Emotional Rational
    Employs Heuristics Doesn’t employ heuristics

Heuristics:

  • A heuristic is a mental shortcut that allows people to solve problems and make judgments quickly and efficiently.
  • Heuristics lead to Cognitive Bias, therefore system 1 of thinking leads to Cognitive Bias

Kahneman and Tversky’s Hospital Study is an example of how people employ heuristics to come to conclusions

People were asked the following question:

The probability that a boy is born at any given point of time is 50%. Given that 15 babies are born in a small hospital per day and 45 babies are born in the larger hospital everyday, which hospital was more likely to record more days where the number of boys born is more than 60%*
A. Larger hospital
B. Smaller hospital
C. Roughly the same

Results

  • 78% of the participants picked option C because of the 50% representative heuristic given in the beginning of the question.
  • The correct answer would be option B - smaller hospital, because in a larger hospital has a larger sample size and is less likely to have disproportionate day.
  • The participants were using representative heuristic

Evaluation

  • This study demonstrates how using one system of thinking can influence our decisions.
  • It is a simple demonstration, but it fails to explain how people would react if they had consequences.

Intuitive thinking leads to biases:

  • The tendency to focus on limited information.
  • The tendency to look for information that confirms pre-existing beliefs.
  • The tendency to avoid holding inconsistent information

Types of biases:

Type of Bias Description
Confirmation Bias Is the tendency to seek out information that confirms pre-existing beliefs
Congruence Bias Is the tendency to seek out positive information rather than useful information
Cognitive Dissonance Inconsistency (dissonance) in thought or action can cause stress, so people tend to either change their behavior or their beliefs.

Studies that can be used to explain Cognitive Bias

  • Wason’s four card problem (Confirmation Bias)
  • Tschirgi’s cake making experiment (Congruence Bias)
  • Leon Festinger’s covert observation of a cult (Cognitive Dissonance)
  • Freedman and Fraser’s safe driving petition experiment (Cognitive Dissonance)
