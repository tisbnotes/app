Flashbulb Memories

  • Brown and Kulik were the psychologists that came up with the theory of flash bulb memories.
  • The theory states that when a high element of surprise and personal consequence is involved, people form vivid memories of the circumstances. These vivid memories are FBMs.
  • While evaluating studies related to FBMs, one of the strengths is that all of them are field experiments and have higher ecological validity, as they were all based on real life situations.

Model of FBM formation

Brown and Kulik’s study

Procedure: They asked participants about their memories of the circumstances of the assassinations of John F. Kennedy and Martin Luther King Jr. Half the participants were Caucasian and the other half were African-american.
Results: The Caucasian participants were more vivid about the circumstances of John F. Kennedy’s assassination, while the African-Americans’ memories about the assassination of King Jr were more vivid.
Evaluation : The reason why Caucasians were more likely to remember the circumstances of Kennedy’s death is because it felt more consequential to them than the death of Martin Luther King jr. The vice versa is true for the African-Americans. The study is simple and looks into the factors help construct an FBM. It is a field experiment. The researchers didn’t directly manipulate the IV, which is the element of surprise and personal consequence experienced by the participants.
The DV is the Vividity of the memories.

Other studies relating to FBMs

Sharot et al - study of personal recollections of 9/11. Biological approach to studying FBMs. Participants were asked to recollect one normal memory and 9/11 memories, under f-MRI machines. 9/11 memories cause selective activation of amygdala. FBMs have unique neural basis.

Neisser et al - study of Loma Prieta earthquake in northern California. Participants were able to accurately recall memories from the incident after 18 months but reported low levels of emotional arousal. Evidence suggested that rehearsal played a more important role in the accurate recall of memory rather than the element of surprise and personal consequence.

Talarico and Rubin - 9/11 study, testing accuracy of recall.
A day after 9/11 attacks, participants were asked to record the circumstances of it. They were tested again on week 1, 6 and 32. repeated measures design. Accuracy of memories declined over time, but perceived accuracy remained high.
