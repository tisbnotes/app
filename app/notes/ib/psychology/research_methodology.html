Quantitiative Research

  • You must operationalise a construct in quantitative studies.
  • Operationalisation - a process of defining the measurement of a phenomenon that is not directly measurable,

Types of Quantitative Studies:

Type of study Description
Experimental Studies Includes one independent variable and a dependent variable, while the other potentially important variables are controlled. Finds cause and effect relationship
Correlational Studies Researcher does not manipulate any variables (there are no IVs or DVs). Variables are measured and the relationship between them is quantified.
Descriptive Studies Relationships between variables are not investigated, and the variables are approached separately. These studies are in form of interviews or surveys and can be used to obtain an in-depth analysis of an area of interest.

Types of Variables

Variable Meaning
Independant The variable that the researcher manipulates to look for a change in the DV
Dependant The variable that changes due to a change in the IV
Control Variables that are kept constant throughout the experiment
Confounding Variables apart from the IV, that may effect the DV

Sampling

  • Sample - The group of people taking part in the experiment
  • Target Population - The group of people to which the findings of the study are expected to be generalized

Types of sampling:

Type Description
Random Participants are chosen at random. Everyone has equal chance of getting selected
Stratified Participants are chosen based on the proportions of people in the target population
Convenience People who are most easily available are chosen
Self-Selected People who self respond to an ad are chosen

Experimental Designs

Independant Measures Design:

  • Involves random allocation of participants into groups and a comparison between these groups. Different participants go through different trials.

Matched Pairs Design

  • Similar to independant measures design.
  • Matching is used to form group

Repeated Measure Design

  • Used when the goes is to compare conditions rather than groups of participants
  • The same group of participants is exposed to two or more conditions and then the conditions are compared

Validity:

Threats to internal validity

1. Selection

  • Occurs if for some reason groups are not equivalent at the start of the experiment
  • We cannot be sure if the post-experiment differences between groups reflect the influence of the IV

2. History

  • Refers to outside events that happen to participants in the course of the experiment
  • These become a problem if they can potentially influence the DV

3. Maturation

  • In the course of the experiment participants go through natural developmental processes, such as fatigue or simply growth

4. Testing Effect

  • The first measurement of the DV may affect the second (and subsequent) measurements

5. Instrumentation

  • Occurs when the instrument measuring the DV changes slightly between measurements

6. Regression to the mean

  • Regression toward the mean is the tendency for scores to average out

7. Experiment mortality

  • Refers to the fact that some participants drop out during an experiment

8. Demand characteristics

  • Refers to a situation in which participants understand the purpose of the experiment and change their behaviour subconsciously to î€žt that interpretation

9. Experimenter bias

  • This refers to situations in which the researcher unintentionally exerts an infuence on the results of the study
  • A double-blind experimental design can be used to combat this.

Qualitative Research

Triangulation

Method Triangulation:

  • The use of different methods in combination to compensate for their individual limitations and reinforce their strenghts

Data Triangulation:

  • Refers to using data from a variety of accessible sources

Researcher Triangulation:

  • Refers to combining observations/interpretations of different researchers

Theory Triangulation:

  • Refers to using multiple perspectives or theories to interpret the data

Rapport

  • Researchers should ensure that participants are being honest
  • The researcher should remind participants about voluntary participation and the right to withdraw so that responses are only obtained from participants who are willing to contribute
  • It should be made clear to participants that there are no right or wrong answers and in general a good rapport should be established

Iterative questioning

  • Spotting ambiguous answers and returning to the topic later while at the same time rephrasing the question

Reflexivity

  • Researchers should reflect on the possibility that their own biases might have interfered with the observations or interpretations

Epistemological Reflexivity:

  • Linked to knowledge of the strengths and limitations of the method used to collect data

Personal Reflexivity:

  • Linked to the personal beliefs and expectations of the researcher

Credibility Checks

  • This refers to checking accuracy of data by asking participants themselves to read transcripts of interviews or field notes of observations and confrm that the transcripts or notes are an accurate representation of what they said (meant) or did

Thick descriptions

  • This refers to explaining not just the observed behaviour itself, but also the context in which it occurred so that the description becomes meaningful to an outsider who never observed the phenomenon first-hand

Dominant respondent bias Occurs in a group interview setting when one of the participants infuences the behaviour and responses of the others
Sensitivity bias The tendency of participants to answer regular questions honestly, but distort their responses to questions on sensitive subjects

Researcher Bias

Bias Description
Confirmation bias Occurs when the researcher has a prior belief and uses the research in an unintentional attempt to conî€žrm that belief
Leading questions bias Occurs when respondents in an interview are inclined to answer in a certain way because the wording of the question encourages them to do so
Question order bias Occurs when responses to one question influence the participantâ€™s responses to the following questions
Sampling bias occurs when the sample is not adequate for the aims of the research
Biased reporting occurs when some findings of the study are not equally represented in the research report

Types of Sampling

Type of Sampling Description
Quota sampling It is decided prior to the start of research how many people to include in the sample and which characteristics they should have
Purposive sampling Similar to quota sampling in the sense that the main characteristics of participants are defined in advance and then researchers recruit participants who have these characteristics
Theoretical sampling This is a special type of purposive sampling that stops when the point of data saturation is reached
Snowball sampling In this approach a small number of participants are invited and asked to invite other people they know who also are of interest for the purposes of the research
Convenience sampling You just use the sample that is easily available or accessible

Qualititative research methods

Observation:

  • The focus of the research is on how people interact, interpret each otherâ€™s behaviour and act upon these interpretations in a natural setting
  • The researched believes that meaningful knowledge in the research area cannot be generated without observation.
  • Observation allows the researcher to become deeply immersed into the studied phenomenon

Types of Observation

Type Description
Naturalistic Observation Observation carried out in naturalistic settings, i.e. in a place that has not been arranged for the purposes of the study
Laboratory Observation Observation carried out in a controlled environment that has been arranged for the purposes of the study
Overt Observation Occurs when participants are aware of the fact that they are being observed
Covert Observation Occurs when participant are not aware of the fact that they are being observed
Participant Observation Researcher joins in and becomes part of the group they are studying to get a deeper insight into their lives
Structured Observation In structured observation information is recorded systematically and in a standardized way
Unstructured Observation Unstructured observations do not have a pre-defined structure and observers simply register whatever behaviours they find noteworthy

Interview

  • This is the only way to get an insight into the nature of subjective experiences and interpretations.
  • Interviews may be used to understand the meanings participants attach to certain events and their points of view.
  • In-depth individual interviews are useful when the topic is too sensitive for people to discuss in a group setting.
Type of interview Description
Structured Interviews Includes a fixed list of questions that need to be asked in a fixed order
Semi-Structured Interviews Do not specify order or a particular set of questions, instead there is a checklist of sorts
Unstructured interview Mostly participant-driven, and every next question is determined by the intervieweeâ€™s answer to the previous one

Focus group

Advantages

  • It is a quick way to get information from several particpants at the same time.
  • It creates a more natural and comfortable environment than a face-to-face, ensuring less participant bias.
  • It is easier to respond to sensitive questions when you are in a group
  • Multiple perspectives are discussed so a more holistic understanding of the topic is achieved

Limitations

  • If one of the participants is especially dominant, this may distort the responses of the other particiapants.
  • It is more difficult to preserve anonymity and confidentiality.
  • Focus group are especially demanding in terms of sampling and creating interview transcripts.

Case study

  • A case study is an in-depth investigation of an individual or a group.
  • The individual or group that is the object of a case study is uniqe in some way. Therefore the purpose is to gain a deep understanding of this particular individial or group.
  • There is less focus on generizability.
  • The case is studied thouroughly, using a combination of different methods.
