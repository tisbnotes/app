Membrane Transport

There are four methods of moving particles across membranes. Apart from these, vesicle movement and transport is important.

Simple Diffusion

  • occurs due the constant random motion of particles in liquids and gases
  • movement of molecules from high concentration to low concentration (down the concentration gradient)
  • passive transport (no input of energy required)
  • Move through membrane without assistance
    E.g. oxygen diffusion in capillaries, urea excretion from cells
Factors Affecting Diffusion Effect
Concentration Gradient Rate increases with greater difference between concentration of two regions
Distance to move Faster rate over shorter distances
Surface Area Greater rate with larger area to diffuse across
Barriers Thicker barriers decrease rate of diffusion
Temperature Greater rate at higher temperatures

Non-polar molecules like oxygen diffuse easily through the membrane.
Small polar molecules like urea can diffuse as well, though slowly.
Large polar molecules with partial charges diffuse at low rates.
Charged particles like ions cannot easily pass through the membrane.

Facilitated Diffusion

  • for particles that cannot easily pass through the membrane directly
  • movement from high concentration to low concentration through a protein
  • channels proteins for inorganic charged particles; carrier proteins for large insoluble lipid molecules
  • passive transport
    Eg. K+ ions in nerve cells, Ca2+ ions for muscle contractions

Proteins tend to be specific to the substrate that it helps move across the membrane.

Facilitated diffusion can be limited by the number of proteins available and present and hence, after a certain point, the rate will not increase despite change in aforementioned factors.

Osmosis

  • diffusion of water molecules from a region of lower solute concentration to higher solute concentration
  • across a partially permeable membrane
  • Osmotic potential: tendency of water to move into a solution - greater solute concentration, greater the osmotic potential
  • occurs due to small size of water molecules that can pass through membrane

Active Transport

  • movement of particles that require energy in the form of ATP
  • against concentration gradient
  • carried out by globular proteins in membranes, called protein pumps
  • molecule or ion enters and binds in central chamber
  • conformational change brought about by hydrolysis of ATP to release energy
  • molecule or ion passes onto desired side of membrane

Example to be known: Sodium Potassium Pumps in Neurons
A nerve impulse is essentially a rapid change in charge in the axons due to movement of sodium and then potassium ions across the axon membrane.

  1. 3 Na+ bind to protein from interior of cell
  2. ATP binds and releases a P to generate conformational change
  3. 3 NA+ released to exterior while 2 K+ bind to protein
  4. P is removed from protein, making it return to original conformation
  5. 2 K+ is released to the interior
  6. Cycle repeats

This is the action of the Sodium Potassium (NA+ K+) pump and not the gated channels.

Bulk Transport

Fluidity of membrane allows materials to be taken into cells through vesicles. Also, the fact that all membranes are of the same structure and composition allows for the seamless merging and pinching of vesicles.

This type of transport does require energy.

A vesicle is a small sac of membrane containing fluid inside.

  • spherical
  • double membrane
  • dynamic structure (constantly constructed, moved, deconstructed)
  • aids in bulk transport, not by molecule basis

Endocytosis

  • taking in of substances into the cell
  • invagination of plasma membrane
  • vesicle formation using double membrane
  • detaches and enters cytoplasm

Pinocytosis : “cell drinking”
uptake of liquids or fine solutions/ suspensions
E.g. uptake in protozoans, some plant cells, some liver cells

Phagocytosis : “cell eating”
engulfing solid material
can be receptor mediated
E.g. feeding in amoeba, phagocytosis by WBCs (neutrophils and macrophages)

Exocytosis

  • secretion : release of material out of the cell
  • movement of vesicle to perimeter
  • bilayer adhesion (vesicle joins the cell membrane)
  • fusion of vesicle and membrane

-> Polypeptides to be secreted are synthesized by ribosomes present on the rER, processed by the Golgi apparatus, packaged and then sent to the membrane in vesicles.

E.g. secretory cells like pancreatic cells, lymphocytes, excretion by unicellular organisms
