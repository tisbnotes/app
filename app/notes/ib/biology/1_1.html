Introduction to Cell Theory

Cell Theory

Cell theory was developed to explain basic features of structure.

  1. All living things are composed of cells.
  2. Cells are the smallest functional unit of life.
  3. New cells arise from pre-existing cells.

The cell theory is a generalization that applies to most but not all organisms.

3 exceptions:

  1. Giant Algae: one multi-nucleated cell that grows to a large size without separate cells.
  2. Striated Muscle Fibres: large multi-nucleated fibre formed from fusion of muscle stem cells
  3. Fungal Hyphae: Aseptate hyphae that do not contain cross walls.

Criteria for Life

All cells show the 7 functions of life.

  1. Metabolism: chemical reactions within cell to release energy in order to sustain life.
  2. Response: the ability to detect and carry out appropriate reaction to stimuli in internal and external environments.
  3. Homeostasis: maintenance of a constant internal environment.
  4. Growth: an irreversible increase in size.
  5. Reproduction: production of offspring.
  6. Excretion: removal of waste products of metabolism and that in excess.
  7. Nutrition: ability to obtain food to provide for energy and materials required.

Surface Area and Volume

Larger surface area to volume ratio is achieved when the cell gets smaller, making diffusion of nutrients and waste materials more effective.

  • Hence, there is a physical limit to size a cell can grow
  • Multi-cellular organisms are the result of working around this limit

Magnification

magnification = (measured length) / (actual length)

Multi-cellularity and Development

Emergent Property: whole is greater than the sum of its parts -> multi cellular organisms have greater properties that arise from interactions of cellular components.

Example:

  1. Biochemical reactions produce emergent property of metabolism
  2. Muscle tissue has properties of contraction and elasticity
  3. Associates to form organs like the heart
  4. Circulatory system has emergent properties of circulation and exchange

Stem Cells

Stem cells are undifferentiated cells characterized by the following:

  1. Self Renewal: ability to divide without specializing
  2. Potency: ability to differentiate into a range of specialized cells

Categories of Stem Cells:

  • Totipotent- can differentiate into all types of cells
  • Pluripotent- can differentiate into majority of cell types
  • Multipotent- can differentiate into a limited number of related cell types

Potency depends on origin of the stem cell: Embryonic Stem Cells (ESCs) are pluripotent > Adult Stem Cells (ASCs) are multipotent

Applications:

  • produce regenerated tissue for skin grafts
  • healing diseases where type of cell is lost (type 1 diabetes)
  • possibility of being used to grow organs
  • renewable source of cells for scientific studies and tests

2 specific cases:

  • Stargardt’s Disease : ESCs injected to replace degenerated retinal cells to restore vision
  • Leukemia: ASCs used to replace mutated and destroyed stem cells in bone marrow killed by chemotherapy.
