5 | Role of government in an economy

Government Aims and Influences

  • The government plays a key role as a producer of goods and services and as an employer. It provides the following goods and services to the general public:
    1. Public Goods
    2. Merit Goods
    3. Public Services
    4. Welfare Services
  • The government influences private producers through the use of regulation, subsidies and taxation policies.
    1. Regulation. Government regulations determine the boundaries within which private producers can operate.
      1. Employment Legislation
      2. Consumer Protection Laws
      3. Environmental Protection
      4. Competition Law
      5. Intellectual Property Rights
    2. Subsidies are financial assistance provided by a government to reduce the costs of production for firms. They are used to encourage output and consumption of certain goods and services.
    3. Tax is a levy or charge imposed by a government to raise costs of production and to reduce consumption of certain goods or services.
  • Governments objectives
    1. Full Employment (low employment)
      • Unemployment refers to people who are out of work, but who are of working age, are mentally and physically able to work, and are actively looking for work.
    2. Price Stability (control of inflation)
      • Inflation refers to the persistent rise in the general price levels in the economy.
      • Higher inflation rates reduces the international competitiveness of a country as prices will be higher. This leads to lower export sales, which can cause potential job losses.
    3. Economic Growth
    4. Redistribution of Income
    5. Balance of Payments Stability
      • The money flowing into a country (sale of exports) is equal to the money flowing out (purchase of exports).
      • A balance of payments surplus is when the inflows exceed the outflows. This is inflationary in the long run due to the excess money entering the country.
      • A balance of payments deficit is when the outflows exceed the inflows. This is when the country has spent more than it has earned.
  • Conflicts between government aims occur. Examples of these are:
    1. Economic Growth vs. Low Inflation
    2. Economic Growth vs. Balance of Payments Stability
    3. Economic Growth vs. Environmental Protection
    4. Economic Growth vs. Redistribution of Income
    5. Full Employment vs. Low Inflation
  • However, it is possible for a government to achieve its macroeconomic objectives without conflicts occurring. For example, it is possible to achieve low inflation along with economic growth through the use of supply-side policies. Similarly, if economic growth is export-led, it will not lead to a deficit on the current account of the balance of payments.

Government Policies

Fiscal Policy

  • It’s the use of taxation and government expenditure strategies to influence the level of economic activity and macroeconomic objectives.
  • If the government
    1. Balances its revenues and its spending, then a balanced budget exists.
    2. Spends more than it collects from its revenues then a budget deficit exists.
    3. Earns more revenue than it spends, a budget surplus exists.

Use of Fiscal Policy

  • Expansionary fiscal policy is used to stimulate the economy by increasing government spending and/or lowering taxes.
  • Contractionary fiscal policy is used to reduce the level of economic activity by decreasing government spending and/or raising taxes.
  • It is also used to redistribute income and wealth in the economy. High income taxes can help reallocate resources from wealthier individuals to poorer ones.
  • Impact on the supply side of the economy:
    1. Lower income tax incentivises people to seek employment and work harder
    2. Government capital expenditure on infrastructure helps to boost capital investment of firms in the economy
    3. Human capital expenditure (government’s expenditure on investing in human education and training) boosts the productivity of labour.
  • Limitations of fiscal policy
    • Time delays
      1. Recognition lags - There is a time lag in governments recognising that intervention is needed to affect the level of economic activity.
      2. Administrative lags - There is a time delay between recognising that fiscal policy intervention is needed and actually implementing the appropriate action.
      3. Impact lags - There is again, you guessed it, time delay between the action of implementing fiscal policy and it actually having an effect on the economy.
    • Conflicting macroeconomic objectives
      • While expansionary fiscal policy helps achieve economic growth, this can fuel demand-pull inflation.
      • While contractionary fiscal policy helps control inflationary pressures, this causes a decrease in real GDP, which can cause unemployment in the economy.

Monetary Policy

  • This is the manipulation if interest rates, exchange rates, and the money supply to control the amount of spending and investment in an economy.
  • It’s hard to directly control the money supply. So, most governments rely on interest rate policy to achieve economic stability.
  • Monetary policy can be used to either expand or contract economic activity in the economy.
    • Expansionary monetary policy increases economic activity by lowering interest rates.
    • Contractionary monetary policy slows down economic activity by raising interest rates.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Time lags, just like fiscal policy
    • Interest rates aren’t the only thing economic activity is dependant on. Even if low interest rates are present, consumer and business confidence levels can impact GDP.
    • Some economists argue that the use of monetary policy can be counterproductive because it restricts economic activity.
      • It discourages foreign direct investment in the country due to high interest rates.
      • It raises the costs of production for firms
      • It can conflict with other macroeconomic objectives like economic growth and employment

Supply Side Policies

  • Policies
    1. Privatisation: The policy of selling off state-owned assets to the private sector if they can be run more efficiently.
    2. Deregulation: The removal of barriers to entry, thereby making markets more competitive and more productive.
    3. Capital Investment: The investment in new technologies, infrastructure and R&D, contributing to the economic development of an economy.
    4. Human Capital Investment: The government’s investment in human training and education improves skills of the country’s workforce, leading to lower unemployment and more labour productivity.
    5. Tax Reforms: Lower taxes can create incentives for work.
    6. Enterprise Zones: The government creates financial incentives for firms to relocate to areas with relatively high rates of unemployment. The incentives include tax rebates and reduced regulations.
  • Advantages:
    1. Improved economic growth
    2. Lower inflation
    3. Lower unemployment
    4. Improved balance of payments
  • Disadvantage:
    1. It takes a ton of time for supply-side policies to be implemented as well as its benefits to be reaped.

Taxation

A tax is a government levy on income or expenditure. Governments impose taxes to:
* Raise government revenue
* Raise the costs of production of demerit products and limit their output
* Charge tariffs on the import foreign goods to encourage consumption on domestic goods.

  • Taxes are levied in two ways:
    • Direct - Taxes taken directly from an individual or business wealth. Examples are taxes on salaries, inheritance and company profits.
    • Indirect - Taxes imposed on expenditure on goods and services. Examples are GST or VAT.
  • The tax burden is the amount of tax that households and firms have to pay. This can be measured (for a country) as a proportion of its GDP.
  • The tax rate is the proportion of an individual’s income that is paid as taxes.
  • Tax avoidance is the legal act of minimising payment of taxes by avoiding spending on items with a large sales tax. On the other hand, tax evasion is the illegal act of not paying the correct amount of tax, perhaps due to a firm under-declaring its profits.

Types of taxation

  • Progressive taxation: Tax rate for an individual rises as their income rises.
  • Regressive taxation: Tax rate for an individual falls as their income rises.
  • Proportional taxation: Tax rate is constant for all individuals.

The impact of taxation:

  1. The imposition of a sales tax will shift the supply curve of a product to the left due to higher costs of production.
  2. Taxation reduces incentives to work and produce, so economic growth takes a hit. This also means that people are disincentivized to get richer, because of progressive taxation.
  3. Taxation reduces the disposable income of individuals and firms, and so reduces inflation.
  4. High corporation taxes and income taxes in a country can reduce foreign direct investment in that country.
  5. Higher taxes on demerit good scan reduce their consumption.
  6. High taxes encourage tax avoidance and tax evasion.
  7. Progressive taxes can help reduce income inequality.
