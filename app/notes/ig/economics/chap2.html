2 | The allocation of resources: how the market works; market failure

Economic systems

Definitions

  • An economic system is the way in which an economy is organised and run, including how best to allocate society’s scarce resources.

Content

  1. Planned economy
    • Features
      1. Production decisions are made by the government.
      2. Resources are controlled by the government on behalf of its citizens.
      3. Production schedules are devised on a long-term basis.
      4. Wage differentials are minimal.
      5. There is minimal engagement in international trade.
    • Advantages
      1. Economies of scale in large state monopolies
      2. No wasteful competition
      3. Social equality, everyone has access to basic resources
      4. Social and environmental protection from negative externalities
      5. Full employment of resources
    • Disadvantages
      1. Lack of economic freedom, ie. lack of choice in goods
      2. Lack of financial incentives to innovate
      3. Inefficiency due to bureaucracy, no entrepreneurial risks are taken by decision makers
  2. Market economy
    • Features
      1. No government interference in economic activities
      2. Market forces of demand and supply are used to allocate resources.
      3. Financial incentives allocate scarce resources.
      4. Competition creates choice and opportunities for firms and private individuals.
    • Advantages
      1. Efficiency due to competition
      2. Freedom of choice
      3. Incentives to work hard
    • Disadvantages
      1. Environmental issues due to negative externalities
      2. Income and wealth inequalities, poverty
      3. Social hardship, lack of public good provision
      4. Wasteful competition
  3. Mixed economy
    • Features
      • Combination of both of the above, this degree is set by the government
    • Advantages
      • Incentives to work hard, to invest, and to save.
      • Large degree of economic freedom
    • Disadvantages
      • Profit motive of private sector businesses leads to high prices
      • Government collects taxes

Demand and supply

Definitions

  • Complements are products that are demanded for their use together with other products.
  • Substitutes are products that are in competitive demand as they can be used in place of each other.

Content

  • Demand
    • The willingness and ability of customers to pay a given price to buy a good or service.
    • Determinants of demand
      • Habits, fashion, and tastes
      • Income
      • Substitutes and complements
      • Advertising
      • Government policies (rules and regulations)
      • Economy (boom vs recession)
      • Size and demographics of the population
  • Supply
    • The willingness and ability of firms to provide goods and services at given price levels.
    • Determinants of supply
      • Costs of production
      • Taxes
      • Subsidies
      • Technological progress
      • Price and profitability of other products
      • Time period
      • Weather
  • Equilibrium price
    • Determined where the demand for a product is equal to the supply of the product
    • Neither excess quantity supplied (oversupply) nor excess quantity demanded (shortage)

Price elasticity

  • Price elasticity of demand (PED)
    • Measures the degree of responsiveness of quantity demanded for a product following a change in its price.
      • PED=% change in quantity demanded% change in price\text{PED} = \large \frac{\text{\% change in quantity demanded}}{\text{\% change in price}}PED=% change in price% change in quantity demanded​
      • PED<1  ⟹  \text{PED} < 1 \impliesPED<1⟹ Inelastic demand
      • PED>1  ⟹  \text{PED} > 1 \impliesPED>1⟹ demand
    • Determinants of PED
      1. Substitution
      2. Income
      3. Necessity
      4. Habits, addictions, fashion, and tastes
      5. Advertising and brand loyalty
      6. Time
      7. Durability
      8. The costs of switching
      9. The breadth of definition of the product.
  • Price elasticity of supply (PES)
    • Measures the responsiveness of the quantity supplied of a product following a change in its price.
      • PES=% change in quantity supplied% change in price\text{PES} = \large \frac{\text{\% change in quantity supplied}}{\text{\% change in price}}PES=% change in price% change in quantity supplied​
      • PES<1  ⟹  \text{PES} < 1 \impliesPES<1⟹ Inelastic supply
      • PES>1  ⟹  \text{PES} > 1 \impliesPES>1⟹ supply
    • Determinants of PES
      1. The degree of spare productive capacity
      2. The level of stocks
      3. The number of producers in the industry
      4. The time period
      5. The easy and cost of factor substitution

Market Failure

Definitions

  • Demerit goods are goods or services which, when consumed, cause negative spillover effects in an economy (e.g. cigarette smoking, alcohol and gambling). Demerit goods are over-consumed due to imperfect consumer information about such goods.
  • Merit goods are goods or services which, when consumed, create positive spillover effects in an economy (e.g. education, training and health care). Merit goods are under-consumed so government intervention is often needed .
  • Free riders are people who take advantage of the goods or services provided by the government but have not contributed to government revenue through taxation.
  • Market failure occurs when the market forces of demand and supply fail to allocate resources efficiently and cause external costs or external benefits.
  • Private Benefit/Cost: benefits/cost the business or consumer receives from producing/consuming the good
  • External Benefit/Cost: advantages/disadvantages society receives due to us of a certain good/service
  • Externalities (or spill over effects) occur where the actions of firms and individuals have either a positive or negative effect on third parties.
    • External benefits are the positive side-effects of production or consumption incurred by third parties, for which no money is paid by the beneficiary.
    • External costs are the negative side-effects of production or consumption incurred by third parties, for which no compensation is paid.
  • A subsidy is a sum of money given by the government to a producer to reduce the costs of production or to a consumer to reduce the price of consumption.

Content

Social costs and benefits

  • Social costs=Private costs+External costs\text{Social costs} = \text{Private costs} + \text{External costs}Social costs=Private costs+External costs
  • Social benefits=Private benefits+External benefits\text{Social benefits} = \text{Private benefits} + \text{External benefits}Social benefits=Private benefits+External benefits
  • Social costs>Social benefits  ⟹  \text{Social costs} > \text{Social benefits} \impliesSocial costs>Social benefits⟹ production is uneconomic
  • Social costs<Social benefits  ⟹  \text{Social costs} < \text{Social benefits} \impliesSocial costs<Social benefits⟹ production is economic

Causes of market failure

  1. Overproduction and overconsumption of goods or services which cause negative externalities on a third party
  2. Underproduction and underconsumption of goods or services which cause positive externalities on a third party
  3. Failure of the private sector to provide public goods due to lack of a profit motive
  4. The existence of a monopoly that charges high prices and exploits customers

Government intervention to solve market failure

  1. Tax on demerit goods
    • Advantages
      1. It increases the price, and therefore decreases the quantity demanded.
      2. Creates tax revenue for government which can be used on other services
    • Disadvantages
      1. Demand may be price elastic, so consumption may not be impacted.
      2. Indirect taxation is regressive, and has a greater impact on low-income earners than high-income earners.
  2. Subsidies for merit goods
  3. Rules and regulations
    • Government bans and legislation can decrease consumption of goods, such as minimum age limits or bans.
    • Advantages
      1. Consumption of the good or service may be reduced.
      2. Awareness of the negative impacts of demerit goods may change long term consumption.
      3. Awareness of the positive impacts of consumption of merit goods is raised.
    • Disadvantages
      1. Restrictions may cause underground illegal markets to develop
      2. Government has no control over underground markets
      3. People break the rules and commit crimes
      4. The fine or punishment for ignoring the rules is hard to enforce and must be set high
  4. Advertising and education
    • Advantages
      1. Behaviour and consumption patterns of individuals and firms may change
      2. Successful advertising may lead to a positive cultural change in the long term
    • Disadvantages
      1. Education and advertising have an opportunity cost for the government
      2. Not all of it is effective
      3. It can take a long time to educate people and for them to accept the message and act on it.
  5. Government provision of public goods
    • Advantages
      1. The goods and services are accessible to all people, regardless of their income or social status.
      2. The consumption of the goods and services has positive externalities as well as private benefits.
    • Disadvantages
      1. Opportunity cost for government
      2. Free goods and services may be over-consumed, so long queues or shortages
      3. In case of shortage, it is hard to determine who should be able to take advantage of the services
      4. Free riders are able to take advantage of free goods and services without contributing to government revenue by paying taxes.
