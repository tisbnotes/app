4 | The private firm as producer and employer

Business Organisation

Types of business organisations

Sole traders

  • A business owned by a single person, also known as a sole proprietorship.
  • Advantages
    1. Can set up business easily, as it is cheap, and very few legal procedures are needed.
    2. Has autonomy in decision making
    3. Keeps all the profits made by the business, incentive to work hard
    4. Enjoys tax advantages
    5. Enjoys privacy
    6. Can be flexible in what the business does
    7. Has a sense of achievement
  • Disadvantages
    1. Bears all the responsibilities for decision making
    2. Has added workload and pressures from having to run the business as a sole trader
    3. Limited specialisation
    4. Limited access to sources of finance
    5. Lack of continuity, business does not run if owner is not present
    6. Restricted ability to exploit economies of scale
    7. Has unlimited liability

Partnerships

  • Legal agreement between two or more people to own/finance/run a business
  • 2-20 partners.
  • Advantages
    1. Far more capital can be raised from partners than sole trader
    2. More ideas and expertise
    3. Specialisation and division of labour
    4. Confidentiality of business affairs
    5. Good working relationships with colleagues and customers due to small nature of firm
    6. More continuity than sole trader
    7. Additional finance can be raised from silent partners.
  • Disadvantages
    1. Disagreements and conflicts between owners may harm operation
    2. All profits must be shared between all owners
    3. All partners except sleeping/silent partners have unlimited liability.

Limited liability companies

  • Board of Directors run the business, who are elected by the shareholders.
  • Advantages
    1. Large amount of finance can be raised from selling shares
    2. Vast sum of money allows them to exploit economies of scale.
    3. Additional finance can be raised through share issue.
  • Disadvantages
    1. Companies have to file financial account and have them audited by external accountants. Time consuming and expensive.
    2. Business affairs are made public.
    3. Administratively difficult, time consuming, and expensive to set up.
    4. Preparation, publication, and distribution of annual reports to shareholders can be expensive.
  • Types
    1. Private limited
      • Only sells shares to people known to existing shareholders
    2. Public limited
      • Shares advertised & sold publicly on stock market through stock exchange to many investors
      • Largest form of business organisation
      • Further drawbacks:
        • High costs in complying with the rules and regulations of stock exchange
        • Potential threat of takeover by rivals who purchase majority stake
        • May suffer from diseconomies of scale
        • Subject to fluctuations in value caused by investor speculation.

Multinationals

  • An enterprise operating in several countries but managed from one (home) country.
  • Advantages
    1. Economies of scale
    2. Job creation in other countries improves standards of living
    3. Larger profit by selling to larger customer base
    4. Spread risks by operating overseas
    5. Avoid trade restrictions and cut down on transport costs by producing in the foreign country
    6. Can move or expand operations in foreign countries to benefit from lower corporation tax rates.
  • Disadvantages
    1. Different legal systems, tax regulations, etc. can hinder smooth running
    2. Size and geographic spread makes it hard to manage the overall business
    3. Fluctuating exchange rates makes measurement of overseas sales and profits hard
    4. Criticism due to cost-cutting practices, poor working conditions and low wages paid.
    5. Often hold power while negotiating with foreign governments due to large revenue
    6. Less competitive local firms may close down

Co-operatives

  • Properties
    • Owned & controlled by its members
    • Each member has an equal share of ownership
    • Worker co-ops are owned & controlled by their workers
    • Consumer co-ops are owned by their consumers
  • Advantages
    1. Limited liability
    2. Workers in worker co-ops take business decisions & share profits
    3. Members of consumer co-ops enjoy profit dividends/lower prices
    4. High staff motivation
    5. Socially responsible principles can increase productivity, morale, and positive externalities.
  • Disadvantages
    1. Many consumer co-ops have been forced out of business by larger companies
    2. Decision making may be slow as all members have the right to contribute
    3. Difficult to settle disputes
    4. Lack of capital as they can’t raise funds through selling shares
    5. No self-interest motivating the members
    6. Only small amount of profits shared, rest is re-invested into the co-op.

Public corporations

  • Owned and controlled by the government
  • Some aim to make a profit while others will deliver public services
  • Advantages
    1. Essential goods and services are provided
    2. Prices are regulated by the government
    3. Unprofitable services can be funded by the government
    4. Can generate revenue for the government
  • Disadvantages
    1. Opportunity cost of using the taxpayers’ money
    2. Inefficiency due to lack of profit motive
    3. May be a monopoly that charges high prices and provides poor service as there is no competition.

Nationalisation

  • The prices of taking assets into state ownership,
  • This is when government takes over private-sector corporations.
  • Reasons for:
    • To control monopolies
    • For safety (e.g. nuclear industry)
    • To protect employment
    • To maintain a public service
    • Private sector organisations will not protect public services & may cut services & raise costs in long run
    • Privatised industries still dominate markets they supply; able to raise prices & cut services
  • Reasons against:
    • If industries are forced to compete, prices will be lower & quality will improve
    • Wider variety of goods
    • Sale of shares raises government revenue & can be used to lower taxes

Production

  • Production refers to the total output of goods and services in the production process.
  • Demand for the factors of production
    • The demand for any factor of production is a derived demand.
    • The demand for factors of production depends on the demand for the goods and services they will be used to produce.
  • Types of production
    • Labour intensive production: The use and cost of labour is proportionately higher than the cost of other factors of production.
    • Capital intensive production: This is when a firm spends more on capital costs than on any factor of production. Potentially huge cost savings in the form of technological economies of scale in the long run.
  • Factors that influence production methods (labour vs capital intensive):
    • Cost of labour compared with the cost of capital
    • The size of the market
    • The firm’s objectives

Productivity

  • Productivity is measure of the degree of efficiency in the use of factor inputs in the production process.
  • Determinants of productivity
    • Investment
    • Innovation
    • Skills and experience
    • Entrepreneurial spirit
    • Competition
  • Consequences of high productivity
    1. Economies of scale
    2. Higher profits
    3. Higher wages
    4. Improved competitiveness
    5. Economic growth

Costs, revenues and profits

  • Costs are the payments made by firms in the production process.
  • Fixed costs are the costs of production that have to be paid regardless of how much a firm produces or sells.
  • Variable costs are the costs of production that change when the level of output changes.
  • Total costs = Fixed costs + Variable costs.
  • Average costs = Total costs ÷ units of output.
  • Revenue = price × quantity sold.
  • Price is also referred to as average revenue.
  • Profit = Total revenue −Total costs.

Objectives

  1. Profit maximisation
    • This is the goal of most private-sector firms.
    • Profits are maximised when the positive difference between a firm’s sales revenues and its costs of production is at its greatest.
  2. Business survival
  3. Gaining market share
  4. Improving image and reputation
  5. Ethical objectives

Perfect competition and monopoly

  1. Perfect competition
    • Features
      1. Many buyers and many sellers
      2. Firms are price takers
      3. No barriers to entry and exit
      4. A homogenous product is produced
      5. Both buyers and sellers have perfect knowledge
  2. Monopoly
    • Features
      1. Single suppliers
      2. Price maker
      3. Imperfect knowledge
      4. High barriers to entry
      5. Tend to use price discrimination
    • Advantages
      • Economies of scale
      • Resources to invest in innovation
      • Potential elimination of wasteful competition
    • Disadvantages
      • Inefficient resource allocation in pursuit of profit maximisation, high prices
      • High barriers to entry prevent competition
      • No substitutes makes demand price inelastic, allowing monopolies to raise prices and exploit consumers
      • Consumers may not make rational choices due to imperfect knowledge.

Business growth

  • Measuring size of firms:
    1. Number of employees
    2. Market share
    3. Capital employed
    4. Sales revenue
  • Way firms can grow
    1. Internal growth (organic growth)
      1. Increasing the number of branches within a country or opening branches in different countries
      2. Franchising - other individuals or firms purchase a license from a firm to trade using its name.
      3. Investment from larger businesses.
    2. External growth (integration/inorganic growth)
      1. Takeovers
      2. Mergers
  • The existence of small firms
    • Small firms have to find a way to compete with supermarkets
    • They provide a range of goods which cannot be bought at the large firm
    • May provide a personalised service
    • Can adapt quickly to changing consumer tastes
    • Focus on smaller markets
    • Cater for specific tastes and for people with higher incomes.

Integration

  1. Horizontal integration
    • Two firms in the same sector of industry integrate together, by either a merger or a takeover.
    • Advantages
      1. Increased market share
      2. Gaining skilled employees from each other
      3. Operating with fewer employees
      4. Economies of scale
    • Disadvantages
      1. Duplication of resources may cause job losses
      2. Possible diseconomies of scale
      3. Culture clash can initially cause communication and organisational problems
  2. Vertical integration
    • Types
      1. Backward: When a firm from one sector of the economy merges with a firm from the previous sector
      2. Forward: When a firm from one sector of the economy merges with a firm from the successive sector
    • Advantages
      1. Quality control of raw materials
      2. No wastage
      3. Price of raw materials falls
    • Disadvantages
      1. Costs of running the entire firm increase
      2. Transport costs that were previously handled externally now have to be managed by the combined firm
  3. Conglomerate integration
    • Firms from different sectors of the industry, which operate in unrelated areas of business, merge or are taken over by another firm.
    • Advantages:
      1. Risk-bearing economies of scale
      2. Diversification
    • Disadvantages
      1. Over-diversification may cause problems with the management of capital and human resources
      2. If a segment of the diversified firm is under-performing, resources may be drained from other areas of the business.

Economies of scale

  • Internal economies: these are cost savings that arise from within the business.
    1. Purchasing/bulk buying
    2. Technical
    3. Financial
    4. Managerial
    5. Risk-bearing
    6. Research and development
    7. Marketing.
  • External economies: arise due to the location of the firm.
    1. Proximity to related firms
    2. Availability of skilled labour
    3. Reputation of the geographical area
    4. Access to transport
  • Diseconomies of scale
    1. Communication issues
    2. Slower decision making
    3. Clash of cultures
    4. Higher labour costs
    5. Lack of motivation in a larger firm
    6. Business may become too diverse
