3 | The individual as producer, consumer and borrower

Money

Definitions

  • Bartering is the act of swapping items in exchange for other items through a process of bargaining and negotiation.
  • A central bank is the monetary authority that oversees and manages the supply of money and the banking system of the nation .
  • Commercial banks are retail banks that provide financial services to their customers, such as accepting savings account deposits and approving bank loans.
  • A stock exchange is an institutional marketplace for training the shares of public limited companies. It provides a platform for individuals, organisations and governments to buy and sell shares.
  • Functions of money describes the role that money plays in the economy: money is a medium of exchange, a store of value and a measure of value (or unit of account).
  • Money is anything that is widely accepted as a means of exchange (and acts as a measure and store of value).
  • Money supply refers to the amount of money in the economy at a particular point in time. A stock exchange is an institutional marketplace for trading the shares of public limited companies.

Labour markets

Definitions

  • Demand for labour is the number of workers firms are willing and able to employ at a given wage rate.
  • Derived demand means the demand for labour is not demanded for itself but for the goods and services labour is used to produce.
  • The equilibrium wage rate is determined when the wage rate workers are willing to work for equals the wage rate that firms are prepared to pay.
  • Geographical mobility occurs when a person is prepared to relocate to another area for a job.
  • The labour force participation rate is the percentage of the working population that is working .
  • Labour supply consists of people who are of working age and are willing and able to work at prevailing wage rates.
  • A national minimum wage is the lowest amount a firm can pay its workers and is set bythe government.
  • Occupational mobility is when a person can easily move from one type of job to another.
  • A salary is a fixed monthly payment in return for labour services.
  • Specialisation of labour occurs when a worker becomes an expert in a particular profession or in a part of a production process.
  • A wage is the return for labour services, paid hourly or weekly. Payment depends on the amount of time worked.

Wage determination

Demand for labour

  • Factors influencing demand for labour:
    1. Aggregate demand in the economy
    2. Increase in productivity of labour
    3. Cost of labour compared with cost of machinery that could replace it

Supply of labour

  • The labour supply in an economy consists of people who are of working age and who are willing and able to work.
  • It can be backward bending as well.
  • Factors affecting supply of labour:
    1. Labour force participation rate
    2. Availability and level of welfare benefits
    3. Changing social climate
    4. Geographical mobility
    5. Occupational mobility

National minimum wage

  • A national minimum wage ( NMW ) is the lowest amount a firm can pay its workers, as set by the government.
  • Advantages
    1. Workers receive a fair wage and are not exploited
    2. Unemployed people have an incentive to work
    3. Low-income earners have more money to spend, increasing aggregate demand in the economy
  • Disadvantages
    1. Increase in the cost of labour for firms, as workers earning higher than minimum might request the firms to raise their wages to maintain a high differential between them and less-skilled workers.
    2. Unemployment may increase as firms’ cost of labour increases, causing them to be able to hire less people.

Differences in earnings

  1. Skilled workers earn more than unskilled workers due to relativity higher demand and relatively lower supply
  2. Male workers earn more than female workers because they do not need to tend after children, but times are a-changing.
  3. In theory, private sector workers can earn more than public sector workers, because of the profit maximisation incentive in private sector firms.
  4. Private sector jobs are less secure than the public sector jobs. The workers in private sector jobs often have to save up for their own pensions in retirement.
  5. The tertiary sector occupations promise more in terms of wage than the other sectors.
  6. The supply of labour of people with special talents or celebrity status is price inelastic. There are very limited number of people with these talents so the wage rate will be very high because of the downward sloping demand curve.

Government interference in a labour market

  1. Employment rights; protect employees from discrimination.
  2. Trade union legislation; governments can reduce the powers of trade unions to make the labour market more efficient.

Specialisation of labour

  • Advantages
    1. Productivity increases
    2. Quality of product increases
    3. Earning potential may increase because of skilfulness
  • Disadvantages
    1. Work becomes repetitive or boring
    2. Workers in low-skilled work may become alienated
    3. The production process may become overdependent on a specific individual
    4. The workers become deskilled in other areas and lose flexibility

Trade Unions

Types of trade unions

  • Craft unions
  • Industrial unions
  • General unions
  • ‘White collar’ unions

The role of trade unions

  • Bargaining with employers for pay rises and better terms and conditions
  • Ensuring that equipment at work is safe to use and that workers are given sufficient training to enable them to perform their role at work safely
  • Ensuring members are given legal advice when necessary
  • Giving support to members when they are made redundant
  • Giving financial and legal support to workers who may have been unfairly dismissed or disciplined
  • Persuading the government to pass legislation in favour of workers, such as laws covering minimum wages, maximum working hours, pension rights and the retirement age.

The basis for wage claims

  • A rise in the cost of living due to inflation
  • Workers in comparable occupations have received a wage raise
  • The increased profits in the industry justify a higher return to labour
  • The productivity of labour has increased, again possibly justifying an increase in wages

Industrial action

  1. Strike: Trade union members refuse to work.
  2. Work-to-rule: Trade union members literally work to fulfil the minimum requirements of their job and will not do anything outside what is written in their contract of employment.
  3. Go-slow: Trade union members complete their work very slowly.
  4. Sit-in: Trade union members turn up to work and occupy the premises but do not undertake their normal work.

Trade unions and the economy

Positive impacts

  • Acts as a channel of communication between employers and employees
  • Offer legal support and advice to employees
  • Negotiate on behalf of their members with employers for better pay and working conditions
  • Negotiate with the government for an increase in minimum wage
  • Improvements in working conditions lead to a better motivated workforce

Negative impacts

  • Industrial action may result in lost productivity
  • Strikes may cause serious disruption to the wider economy
  • Workers do not get paid when they do not work, so they lose wages and earnings. Living standards fall.
  • Trade unions increase the costs to firms and reduce profits

Trade union membership

Causes for increasing trade union membership

  • Growth in manufacturing jobs
  • More workers experiencing low pay and poor working conditions in manufacturing
  • Increasing wealth gap and higher costs of living

Causes for declining trade union membership

  • Changing government legislation which reduces union influence
  • Decline in manufacturing
  • Growth in part-time employment
  • Firms independently agreeing fair terms and conditions with their employees without negotiating with trade unions
  • Increase in unemployment
  • Increase in employment in small firms
  • Increase in number of self-employed people

Income and expenditure

  • *Disposable income refers to the income earned by an individual after income tax and other charges have been deducted.
  • The wealth of an individual is measured in by the amount of assets they own minus their liabilities (what they owe).
  • Conspicuous consumption occurs when people purchase goods and services that they feel increase theirs status or image.

Expenditure patterns

  • Determinants of consumer spending:
    • Income
      • Low income group
        • Spending: mainly on necessities. high %.
        • Saving: low
        • Borrowing: to fund expenditure on capital items, sometimes on necessities. Less likely to be lent to as they represent high risk.
      • Middle income group
        • Spending: necessities and on some luxuries. lower %.
        • Saving: some money is saved, medium %
        • Borrowing: borrow to fund capital expenditure, take out mortgages.
      • High income group
        • Spending: purchase luxury goods and services. small % of income.
        • Saving: high %.
        • Borrowing: less need to borrow to fund capital expenditure. Banks lend easily as they represent low risk.
    • Inflation, as it controls the purchasing power of individuals
    • Interest rates
      • With an increase, cost of borrowing increases and return on saving increases
    • Confidence levels
    • Age
    • The size of households

Saving

  • Saving occurs when a person puts away part of their current income for future spending.
  • Reasons to save:
    1. Sacrifice current spending for more future spending
    2. To earn interest
    3. Precautionary reasons
  • Determinants of the level of saving:
    1. Age
    2. Attitude towards saving
    3. Consumer and business confidence
    4. Interest rates

Borrowing

  • Borrowing occurs when an individual, firm or the government takes out a loan from a bank or financial institution, paying it back over a period of time with interest.
  • Reasons to borrow:
    • To fund expensive items
    • To purchase property or land
    • To start up a new business
    • To fund large projects like business expansions
    • To fund current expenditure in the event of an emergency
  • Factors that affect level of borrowing:
    • Availability of funds
    • Credit cards
    • Store cards
    • Wealth
