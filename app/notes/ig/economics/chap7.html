7 | Developed and developing economies: trends in production, population and living standards

Standards of living

  • Standard of living refers to the social and economic wellbeing of individuals in a country at a point in time.
  • Indicators of standards of living:
    • GDP per capita
    • The cost of living
    • Material wealth
    • Health indicators
    • Education indicators
    • Social and environmental indicators

Development

  • Low and middle income countries are called “developing countries” or less economically developed countries (LEDC)s.
    • Characteristics
      1. Low GDP per capita
      2. Low life expectancy
      3. Low literacy rates
      4. High population growth
      5. Poor infrastructure
      6. Low foreign direct investment
      7. Poor health care
      8. Low labour productivity
      9. High public debt (money owed by the government)
      10. Reliance on primary sector output
      11. Corruption
  • High income countries are called “developed countries” or more economically developed countries (MEDC)s.

Poverty

  • Poverty is a condition that exists when people lack adequate income and wealth to sustain a basic standard of living.
    • Hunger and malnutrition
    • Ill health and mortality from illness
    • Limited or lack of access to education and other basic services
    • Homelessness and inadequate housing
    • Unsafe environments
    • Social discrimination and exclusion.
  • Absolute poverty
    • Exists when there is extreme, outright poverty.
    • Poverty in absolute poverty are undeniably poor.
    • Income, if any, is spent on minimal basic human needs for survival.
    • To measure, the number of individuals living below a certain level of income called the poverty line is calculated.
  • Relative poverty
    • This is a comparative measure, rather than an absolute measure, of poverty.
    • These people have a lower standard of living in comparison with the average member of society.
    • Relative poverty measures the extent to which a person’s financial resources fall below that of the average income for the population.

Policies to alleviate poverty

  • Fiscal policies
    1. Progressive tax systems
    2. Government transfer payments, ie. unemployment benefit, state pension funds, child benefit.
    3. Government provision of basic services.
  • Monetary policies
    1. Low interest rates to encourage borrowing
    2. Low exchange rates for greater export sales
  • Supply side policies
    1. Policies to reduce unemployment
    2. Policies to increase the quantity and quality of education
    3. Sustained economic growth
    4. Labour market reforms
  • Foreign aid
    1. Bilateral foreign aid: assistance from one government to the other
    2. Multilateral foreign aid: official financial assistance from more than one source
    3. Development aid: official financial assistance from governments and agencies to fund the economic development of a country
    4. Humanitarian aid: philanthropic financial assistance for saving lives and maintaining human dignity.

Population

  • Factors that affect population growth
    1. Birth rate
    2. Death rate
    3. Fertility rate
    4. Net migration rate
    5. Life expectancy
    6. Social changes
    7. Natural disasters, diseases, and war

Population distribution

  • Gender
    • Normally, gender split is even.
  • Age distribution
    • LEDCs
      • Relatively large proportion of population in the younger age groups
      • Population rapidly decreases as age increases
    • MEDCs
      • Ageing population
      • Growing number of elderly people
      • Population highest at the middle ages
  • Dependency ratio
    • Dependency ratio = dependent population ÷ working population
    • Higher the dependency ration, the greater the tax burden on the working population to support those who are not economically active.
    • Causes of a high dependency ratio:
      1. High birth rates, mainly in LEDCs
      2. Higher compulsory school leaving age, keeping students dependent for longer
      3. Social changes such as demand for higher education or more people choosing early retirement

The optimum population

  • The optimum population exists when the output of goods and services per head (GDP per capita) of the population is maximised.
  • A country is under populated if it does not have sufficient labour to make the best use of its resources.
  • A country is over populated if the population is too large, given the available resources of the country.

Consequences of population changes

  • As a country develops, there is a shift away from reliance on primary and secondary sector production towards tertiary output.
  • As a population grows:
    • The demand for goods and services change with variations in population distribution.
    • The demand for, and supply of, labour will change following long-term changes in population trends.
    • A growing population brings about more benefits as the government is able to collect more tax revenues from a larger workforce.
    • There is added pressure on the government to provide more public services, welfare benefits, and state pensions.
    • More pressure is put on an economy’s scarce resources. This can lead to inflationary pressures or an increase in demand for imports.
    • Natural and non-renewable resources are depleted, and this puts strain on the natural environment.
